The empty-chair frame's value depends on its generativity. If naming absent-party representation as the organizing principle behind the FS AI RMF produces only a more sympathetic vocabulary for compliance theater, the frame has earned nothing. If it produces interpretive leverage on specific controls — vocabulary for talking about what a control is for, criteria for assessing whether an implementation represents the parties the control was written to protect, diagnostic moves available to examiners reviewing the implementation — the frame has earned its position as an organizing principle. This section demonstrates the latter through a worked example in BSA/AML transaction monitoring.

We choose BSA/AML rather than lending or underwriting deliberately. Lending is where the empty-chair frame is most obvious — the loan applicant is concrete, the harm is concrete, fair-lending controls have decades of regulatory history to draw on, and the absent party's interest is well-rehearsed. The frame applied to lending tends to confirm what readers already believe, which is a poor test of whether the frame is doing work or merely renaming work that was already being done. BSA/AML is harder. The empty chairs are plural and conflicting in ways lending is not. The institution's interest is more aligned with the regulator's than in lending. The customer's interest is ambiguous — protection from fraud and protection from false flagging point in opposite operational directions. If the frame produces clarity here, where the chairs disagree and the controls do not align cleanly with any single absent party, the frame is doing more than renaming.

## The empty chairs in BSA/AML monitoring

Six absent parties have interests in how a community bank monitors transactions for suspicious activity. We enumerate them not because all six receive equal weight but because naming them explicitly is itself the frame's first move. Compliance theater operates by leaving the chair count implicit, allowing institutions to claim representation of one chair while structurally serving another.

*The next victim of fraud whose pattern this transaction is part of.* When a transaction is part of an emerging fraud pattern, the institution's monitoring decision affects not the customer making the transaction but the customer who will be victimized in the next iteration. The next victim is fully absent from the transaction record and from the institution's customer relationship. They are represented in the monitoring architecture or they are not represented at all.

*The customer whose account is frozen on a false positive.* Transaction monitoring with high sensitivity produces false positives at scale. The false-positive customer experiences the monitoring as adversarial — their transaction is rejected, their account flagged, their access disrupted, and the explanation is typically inadequate or absent. They are present in the transaction record but absent from the decision context that determined how their pattern was scored.

*The marginalized customer whose transaction patterns are flagged at elevated rates.* Models trained on historical SAR data inherit historical flagging biases. Communities whose transaction patterns differ from the model's training distribution — recent immigrants using money-transfer services, cash-economy participants, members of religious or cultural communities with non-standard remittance patterns — experience flagging rates uncorrelated with their actual involvement in suspicious activity. Their absence from the decision context is structural: the model was not trained to represent them as a separate population.

*The institution itself, in the form of its future enforcement exposure.* The bank that fails to file required SARs faces regulatory action; the bank that over-files faces examiner skepticism about its risk calibration. Both are forms of enforcement exposure that compound over time. The institution's future self — the bank in five years, when current monitoring decisions become the subject of an examination — is an empty chair the present institution can either represent through architectural attestation or fail to represent through reliance on reconstructed reasoning.

*The financial system, in the form of monitoring quality across institutions.* BSA/AML is a coordinated regulatory regime; one institution's monitoring inadequacy creates externalities for institutions whose monitoring is more rigorous, because criminals route around weak links. The system as a whole is an empty chair represented by inter-agency information sharing, FinCEN reporting, and cross-institution examination practice. The community bank's monitoring architecture either contributes to this representation or free-rides on it.

*The criminal whose laundering depends on monitoring inadequacy.* This is the empty chair the regulatory regime is designed to *not* represent. We name it explicitly because its absence in the architecture is intentional and structural, and because conflating "we should not represent the criminal" with "we should not consider the criminal's interest in our threat model" is a category error. The criminal's interest is the threat the architecture is designed against. The frame requires naming the chair we are designing to exclude.

The 230 control objectives do not talk about controls this way. They specify what the institution must do — record-keeping, suspicious-activity reporting, customer due diligence, transaction monitoring thresholds — without naming whose interest each requirement structurally protects. The empty-chair frame's first contribution is the vocabulary that makes this structure visible: each control's substance is determined by which empty chairs it represents and which it does not.

## Walking three controls through the frame

Consider three FS AI RMF control objectives that touch transaction monitoring in different ways. We do not specify the exact control numbers — the framework's structure is more stable than its enumeration, and tying the analysis to specific numbers makes this section brittle. The substantive content is what matters.

*Control: monitoring system performance is continuously assessed for drift in detection rates and false-positive rates, with documented thresholds triggering revalidation.*

The empty-chair reading: this control protects the institution's future self (drift detection prevents enforcement exposure from gradually degraded monitoring), protects the next victim of fraud (drift detection ensures detection rates remain adequate), and partially protects the marginalized customer (false-positive drift surfaces disparate impact over time, if the false-positive rate is decomposed by population rather than aggregated). Implementation choices determine which empty chair is actually represented. A system that monitors aggregate false-positive rates without decomposition by population represents the institution and the next victim but does not represent the marginalized customer; the false-positive rate can remain stable in aggregate while shifting in distribution across populations. The control's text is satisfied; the marginalized customer's chair remains empty. An implementation that monitors decomposed false-positive rates and triggers revalidation on disparate-impact drift represents the marginalized customer; the implementation choice is the difference between a control that does its work and a control that performs compliance.

This is the frame's first interpretive move: the control's text underspecifies which empty chairs it represents, and the implementation choice is where the substantive question lives. Asking *which empty chair does this implementation represent* is more diagnostic than asking *does this implementation comply with the control text*.

*Control: AI components used in transaction monitoring have documented inventory entries including model purpose, training data provenance, and validation methodology.*

The empty-chair reading: this control protects the institution's future self (an examiner arriving in three years can reconstruct what the system was), protects the future compliance officer (institutional knowledge survives personnel turnover), and indirectly protects the marginalized customer (training data provenance is the precondition for assessing whether the model represents their population adequately). The control does not directly protect the false-positive customer or the next victim; their interests are downstream of model performance, which the inventory does not directly assess.

The implementation question is what counts as adequate documentation. A model card listing high-level capabilities and a training-data summary protects no empty chair effectively — it produces a documented inventory entry without producing the institutional self-knowledge the inventory is for. An entry that captures specific decisions during model development, the empty chairs considered in those decisions, the trade-offs made, and the institutional context in which the model was approved produces inventory that the future compliance officer can actually use. The first is theater; the second is representation. Both satisfy the control text. The frame produces the distinction the text does not.

*Control: human review processes for AI-flagged transactions include documentation of reviewer reasoning sufficient for later examination.*

The empty-chair reading: this control reaches for the dishonest middle. The text frames human review as the verification regime; the reviewer's documented reasoning is treated as the audit trail. The empty chairs the control is meant to protect — the false-positive customer, the next victim, the institution's future self — are represented only if the documented reasoning actually captures what the reviewer did. This is where the second categorical from the opening becomes operational: documented reasoning is not verification of reasoning unless the documentation binds to what the reviewer actually thought, and reviewer self-reports are systematically poor at this binding under time pressure and case volume.

The implementation question is whether the review process produces reasoning that is contemporaneous and auditable, or reasoning that is reconstructed and narrative. A review interface that captures decision context as the reviewer engages with it — what was consulted, what alternatives were considered, what made the difference — produces contemporaneous evidence that represents the empty chairs. A review process that asks the reviewer to write a justification after deciding produces reconstructed narrative that satisfies the control text without representing the empty chairs. The control underspecifies; the frame makes the underspecification visible.

## What the frame produced

Three moves, in this section, the checklist did not produce. First, an explicit accounting of which absent parties have interests in BSA/AML monitoring, including the chair we are designing to exclude. Second, a vocabulary for distinguishing controls by which empty chairs their implementations represent, separating control text from implementation substance. Third, a diagnostic question — *which empty chair does this implementation represent* — that produces leverage on assessment that the compliance question — *does this implementation satisfy the text* — does not.

These are not solutions to the problems the controls address. They are a frame for thinking about the controls that produces interpretive and diagnostic moves the framework's checklist surface does not provide. The frame's claim to organizing principle status rests on whether moves of this kind generalize across controls and across regulated decision contexts, which the remainder of this paper develops. What this section establishes is that the moves exist — that the frame produces structure where the checklist produces only enumeration.
