Section 2 demonstrated that the empty-chair frame produces interpretive structure where the FS AI RMF's checklist surface produces only enumeration. This section makes a stronger move: it argues that AI applications in regulated decision contexts divide into two structurally different categories, distinguished not by risk magnitude but by whether a verification regime is *available* for the application's outputs. The categoricals from the opening — post-hoc explanation as structurally inadequate, human oversight as not a verification regime when reviewing explanations — are taxonomic markers rather than empirical observations. They identify where AI applications cross a structural line, after which the verification problem has not been solved by any deployed architecture we know of.

The worked example is risk-based pricing in consumer lending — the determination of the interest rate, fees, and terms offered to an approved applicant on the basis of their predicted default probability and the institution's pricing strategy. We choose pricing rather than denial because the empty chair in pricing is sharper than in denial: the priced applicant typically receives no explanation of why their rate is what it is, has no regulatory entitlement equivalent to an adverse action notice, and has no comparison set against which to evaluate whether their rate is fair. The customer is structurally placed in a position where they cannot challenge the pricing because they cannot see it, and the institution reads the customer's acceptance of the rate as evidence the rate was fair. This is produced absence at scale, in the regulatory space the frame is designed to address.

## The structural line

Recall the opening's framing: a verification regime is the set of mechanisms by which parties not present at the moment of decision can later assess whether their interests were represented in the reasoning, not merely in the outcome. The categoricals identify two failure modes for verification regimes built on text-channel observation:

First, post-hoc explanation methods do not bind to the underlying reasoning of the model. The explanation is generated by a separate process — feature attribution, counterfactual generation, attention visualization — that produces text correlated with the model's output but not constituting the model's reasoning [Rudin 2019, Bordt et al. 2022]. The empty chair receives an artifact that resembles reasoning without being reasoning. Verification of that artifact is not verification of the model.

Second, human review of an explanation is review of the artifact, not the underlying process. A reviewer who reads a pricing recommendation and signs off on the underwriting file has verified that the recommendation appears reasonable, that no obvious anomalies appear, that the file is well-formed. The reviewer has not verified that the rate offered reflects the applicant's actual creditworthiness rather than the pricing model's idiosyncrasies, because the model's reasoning is not present in the file being reviewed [Lanham et al. 2023, Turpin et al. 2023].

Together these foreclose a category of architectural claim. An AI pricing application that generates a rate, attaches a post-hoc justification, and is signed by a human underwriter does not have a verification regime for the empty chair the rate is communicated to. The rate is communicated; the rate is not justified; the customer is not equipped to interrogate whether the rate reflects creditworthiness or model artifact. The empty chair has not received verification.

This is a structural foreclosure, not a quantitative one. No amount of additional human review, additional pricing-tier disclosure, or additional documentation closes the gap, because the gap is in the binding between the priced rate and the reasoning that produced it, which the architecture does not provide.

## Risk-based pricing as the produced-absence canonical case

Risk-based pricing in consumer lending serves a defensible economic function: matching the price of credit to the predicted risk of default, allowing creditors to extend credit to applicants who would be denied under uniform pricing while charging rates that reflect the additional risk. The mechanism is straightforward in principle. The applicant's predicted default probability determines a rate; the rate is offered; the applicant accepts or declines.

The empty chair the regime should address is the priced applicant. Unlike the denied applicant, the priced applicant has no regulatory entitlement to a substantive explanation of their rate. The Truth in Lending Act requires disclosure of the rate's mathematical structure (APR, finance charges, payment schedule); it does not require disclosure of why this particular rate was offered to this particular applicant. The applicant receives a rate; the applicant accepts or declines; the institution proceeds.

The dishonest middle in pricing is more diffuse than in denial because there is no regulatory artifact equivalent to the adverse action notice. The pricing decision is not communicated to the customer as a reasoned determination; it is communicated as a take-it-or-leave-it offer. The institution's position is that the customer's acceptance constitutes assent. The customer's position is that they took what was offered because they needed credit and had no basis for negotiation.

This is produced absence at architectural scale. The customer who would have challenged the rate if challenge were a thing they expected to receive does not challenge, because the architecture of risk-based pricing does not contemplate challenge as a normal part of the transaction. The institution reads the customer's silence — accepted offers, completed transactions, regulatory examinations finding no fair-lending issues — as evidence that pricing is fair. The architecture has produced the silence; the institution interprets the silence as endorsement.

The frame's diagnostic move on this configuration: *what is the architecture doing that creates this silence, and what is it inferring from the silence it created?*

The architecture is doing several things. It is producing pricing decisions through models whose reasoning is not communicated to the customer. It is structuring the transaction such that the customer cannot effectively negotiate without comparable offers, which they typically cannot obtain without applying to multiple institutions and incurring multiple credit pulls. It is producing aggregate pricing data that institutions analyze for fair-lending compliance, while the data itself is censored — the customers who declined offers do not appear in pricing data the same way as those who accepted, and the customers who would have asked for justification if justification were available do not exist as a population the institution measures.

The institution then infers from this silence that pricing is fair. The customers who accepted are taken as evidence the rates were acceptable. The customers who declined are taken as having self-selected out of the transaction. The customers who never applied because they expected unfavorable rates are not measured at all. The architecture has produced a population of accepted-and-silent customers and reads their silence as evidence of pricing fairness.

## The taxonomic move

The categoricals divide AI applications in pricing into two categories, and the empty-chair frame makes the division visible.

*Applications where verification is structurally available.* AI surfacing pricing anomalies for human review — applications where a model identifies cases that deviate from expected pricing patterns and a human underwriter reasons through whether the deviation is justified. The human's reasoning is what the institution can verify; the AI's role is bounded to surfacing. The empty chair (the priced applicant) is represented through the human's substantive engagement with the pricing decision, which can be made auditable by contemporaneous capture.

*Applications where verification is not structurally available.* Pricing models that produce a rate, attach a post-hoc justification (typically as Shapley-value attribution to underwriting features), and are approved by a human underwriter via signature on the underwriting file. The model decides the rate; the explanation method produces text; the human signs. No party has produced reasoning that survives examination. The institution has produced a rate, a documentation artifact, and a signature.

These are not gradations along a single axis of risk. They are architecturally different. The first is AI as decision-support tool with human reasoning as the locus of accountability; the second is AI as decision-maker with no locus of accountability the empty chair can interrogate. The line is structural.

A common rejoinder: "pricing models are statistically validated against outcomes — default rates by pricing tier, fair-lending disparate-impact analysis, ongoing monitoring." This is true and important, and it is not a verification regime for the empty chair we are discussing. Statistical validation against outcomes verifies that the model performs as intended in aggregate; it does not verify that any individual customer's rate reflects their creditworthiness rather than model artifact. The empty chair is the individual priced applicant, not the pricing model in aggregate. Aggregate statistical validation is a verification regime for the institution's risk management; it is not a verification regime for the customer's interest in their own rate.

Another rejoinder: "pricing in lending has always been opaque; the customer has never had access to underwriter reasoning even in the era of human pricing." This is also true and also not what the categoricals foreclose. Pre-AI pricing was opaque, but the human underwriter who made the pricing decision had reasoning that, in principle, could be examined. Examination was difficult but not structurally foreclosed. AI pricing introduces a different problem: the model's reasoning cannot be examined even in principle, because the explanations attached to it are post-hoc constructions rather than the model's reasoning itself. The opacity is not continuous with pre-AI opacity; it is structurally different.

## What the categoricals foreclose, and what they do not

The categoricals foreclose architectures that rely on post-hoc explanation as a verification regime in regulatory contexts where the empty chair has interest in the reasoning behind a decision affecting them. They do not foreclose AI in pricing. They foreclose specific architectural patterns:

- Pricing models whose decisions are communicated via rates without substantive reasoning, with human underwriter signatures attesting to review of the file rather than verification of the rate's basis. The empty chair is structurally produced-absent.

- Documentation regimes where the underwriter's "reasoning" is post-hoc justification of the model's recommendation rather than independent engagement with the pricing decision. The accountability locus is fictional.

- Aggregate validation regimes treated as verification of individual pricing decisions. The aggregate empty chair (the regulator examining fair-lending compliance) is partly represented; the individual empty chair (the priced applicant) is not.

The categoricals do not foreclose:

- Ante-hoc interpretable pricing models whose reasoning is structurally available for inspection by underwriter, regulator, and (in principle) customer. The model's decision and the model's reasoning are the same artifact.

- Decision-support tools where AI surfaces pricing material for human reasoning, the human reasons independently about the rate offered, and the reasoning is captured contemporaneously.

- Architectures that bind explanations to underlying reasoning through observable channels, where the binding can be examined rather than asserted. These are the work of Paper 2.

The pricing example demonstrates the categoricals at a regulatory location where the dishonest middle is sustained primarily through produced absence rather than through inadequate explanation regimes. The empty chair is not addressed by an artifact at all; the empty chair is structurally placed in a position where they cannot interrogate the decision affecting them, and the institution reads their silence as endorsement. The frame's contribution is making the production of silence visible — separating the architecture's interpretation of customer acceptance from the conditions under which that acceptance occurred.

The remaining sections develop the diagnostic techniques that operationalize this taxonomic move (Section 4) and the categories of architectural primitive that empty-chair representation requires (Section 5). The frame's claim is that an honest AI governance framework must hold the structural line, even when the regulatory regime currently does not require holding it. Pricing is a regulatory space where the empty chair has been produced-absent by the regulatory regime itself; an honest framework recognizes this and proposes architectural primitives that represent the chair the regulation does not currently require representing.
