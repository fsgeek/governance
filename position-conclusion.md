The Financial Services AI Risk Management Framework arrived in February 2026 with 230 control objectives and no interpretive frame for what they collectively protect. Read as a checklist, the framework is operationally daunting and conceptually opaque. Read as a structure, it has an underlying coherence — empty-chair representation — that makes both interpretation and operationalization tractable, and that gives examiners diagnostic leverage the checklist does not provide.

We have argued for three positions in this paper, each of which constrains the others.

The first is that AI governance frameworks in regulated decision contexts are coherent only insofar as they make absent parties audible to the parties present at the moment of decision. The empty chairs are plural and they do not always agree. But the architectural function of regulatory governance is to give them voice in the institutional mechanism, not merely in the institutional documentation. Controls that induce observable, auditable constraints on system behavior or evidence production represent absent parties; controls that assert representation without inducing constraint are decoration. This distinction is not stylistic. It is the difference between governance and governance theater.

The second is that post-hoc explanation is structurally inadequate as a verification regime in adversarial contexts, and bank examination is structurally adversarial. Any architecture that relies on explanation-of-output to verify the reasoning of an AI system in a high-stakes regulated decision has built a Potemkin form of accountability. The empty chair has not been represented; an explanation has been produced and signed. These are not equivalent, and the regulatory apparatus increasingly recognizes that they are not. Frameworks built on the equivalence will not survive the recognition.

The third is that human oversight of AI-generated decisions, in the form currently common in vendor offerings, does not address the verification gap. A human who reviews an explanation has not verified the reasoning. A signature attests to review; it does not attest to verification. The org-chart picture of accountability has been preserved; the empty chair has been moved further from the room, not closer to it. This is the dishonest middle, and its commercial attractiveness will not protect it indefinitely from regulatory scrutiny that learns to ask what an honest verification regime actually requires.

These positions narrow the design space for AI in community banking but do not close it. AI applications in which the verification regime is structurally available — pattern recognition surfacing cases for human investigation, anomaly detection, consistency monitoring across decision histories — remain available and valuable. Applications in which the verification regime is absent require either architectural innovation that closes the gap or honest acknowledgment that the application is not yet ready for deployment in regulated decision contexts. The line between these is not a matter of risk tolerance. It is a matter of whether absent parties are represented or not, and the regulatory apparatus exists precisely to enforce that distinction.

The architectural primitives that empty-chair representation requires — tamper-evident temporal capture, evidence binding, three-dimensional confidence characterization, structural drift typology, diagnostic techniques applied to the attested record — are sketched in this paper at the property level and left to follow-on work for implementation specification. Our concern here has been with the frame, not the build. But the frame's value is in the architecture it forces honest implementations toward, not in any claim that current implementations have arrived. Community banks deploying AI under the FS AI RMF face a genuine question about what *commensurate* compliance means for their context. The frame we have proposed provides the interpretive substrate for answering it. The answer itself remains to be built, and the building is governed by the same structural commitments the frame requires.

The empty chairs are watching. The paper's claim is that we should architect for them deliberately, rather than leave them at the mercy of compliance theater.
