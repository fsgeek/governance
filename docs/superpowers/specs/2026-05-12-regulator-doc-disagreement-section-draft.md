## §B — Within-Rashomon disagreement as a supervisory signal *(drafted section)*

### B.1 What the disagreement is

When a tier's policy-admissible refinement set (§A.2) contains more than one materially different model, those models *disagree* about which borrowers inside the tier are higher-risk. On the LendingClub 2015 vintages, this happens on the tiers where the within-tier residual is multi-factor: e.g. in one mid-prime tier (grade B1), the admissible refinements rank the tier's borrowers with a pairwise rank-correlation as low as ~0.17 — i.e. policy-admissible refinements of that tier *strongly disagree* about the within-tier risk ordering. On tiers where the residual is single-factor, the set collapses — every "member" is essentially the same model in different feature-subset clothing, and there is nothing to adjudicate. So the first thing this signal reports is binary-per-tier: **does the documented policy admit refinements that disagree about within-tier risk, or not?**

### B.2 What the refinements disagree *about* — and it is legible, and tier-specific

Where the set is plural, the disagreement is not a black-box quantity. For each tier, the per-borrower disagreement is well-approximated by a *three-question decision tree* over one or two of the policy's named features (out-of-sample, the approximation captures roughly half to four-fifths of the variation in the disagreement) — so an examiner can be told, in one sentence, *which* feature the admissible refinements are fighting over and *where*. Two specifics worth stating plainly:

- **The contested feature is different in different tiers.** In the LendingClub 2015 data: in two of the plural tiers the refinements disagree over *debt-to-income*; in two others over *income*; in one over *FICO*. Notably, in the most-disagreeing tier (B1) the contested feature is **income** — the admissible refinements disagree most about the *lowest-income* borrowers within that tier. So "the refinements disagree" is **not one portfolio-level phenomenon** — it is a per-tier fact about which residual feature that tier's admissible refinements happen to carve differently. Report it tier by tier ("in tier X the policy underdetermines the income-effect for low-income borrowers"), never aggregated.
- **The disagreement concentrates at the extremes.** Within the contested feature, the refinements disagree most about borrowers at the *tails* (very high DTI, or very low income, etc.) and least about borrowers in the middle — consistent with the refinements agreeing on the tier's base rate for typical borrowers and only diverging at the edges of the feature range.

### B.3 What this is good for in supervision — aggregate, tier-by-tier model-risk surveillance

The supervisory value is this: **when a tier's policy-admissible refinements disagree substantially, the documented policy does not pin down the within-tier risk ordering** — the price the bank charges for that tier is, *within the policy's own stated vocabulary*, underdetermined. That is an auditable fact, and it is one a post-hoc attribution method structurally cannot surface (there is no "set of admissible models" for it to disagree, no notion of the policy underdetermining anything — SHAP attributes a single model's output, full stop). It falls out of the policy being *documented and first-class*. An examiner can use it to focus attention: the tiers where the admissible refinements disagree most are the tiers where the bank should be asked to show its basis (see §B.5).

### B.4 What this is **not** good for — case-level triage *(the tested-and-rejected use; state it explicitly)*

It is natural to suppose that the *borrowers* the admissible refinements most disagree about are the borrowers a decision system should route to a human reviewer rather than auto-decide. **This was tested directly — on a held-out quarter, on the tiers where the disagreement is real — and it does not hold.** The high-disagreement borrowers are *not* the ones where the consensus prediction is least reliable; if anything they are slightly *more* predictable and better-calibrated. The reason (confirmed by the geometry result, §B.2): per-borrower disagreement tracks *where the within-tier residual signal is*, not *where the models are confused* — the refinements diverge where there is structure to carve differently, which tends to be where there is *more* to predict, not less. **Consequently: do not use refinement-disagreement as an auto-decide-vs-route trigger.** It is a *tier-level surveillance* signal, not a *case-level triage* signal. (One narrow legitimate case-level use: if a decision is *already* under human review for an independent reason — a borrower complaint, an adverse-action appeal, an audit sample — then showing the reviewer the two most-disagreeing admissible refinements and the feature they fight over is a useful way to frame the residual judgement the reviewer faces. But the disagreement does not *choose* the review queue.) We document this as a tested-and-rejected design alternative rather than omitting it, because the temptation to use it that way is real and the rejection is informative.

### B.5 What an examiner does with it, concretely

Given a bank's documented underwriting/pricing policy (in machine-readable form) and loan-level outcomes:

1. For each risk tier, construct the policy-admissible refinement band (§A.2).
2. Report, per tier: whether the band is plural; if so, a disagreement magnitude (the worst pairwise rank-correlation among admissible refinements), the contested feature, and the region of that feature where the disagreement concentrates.
3. Flag the tiers with large disagreement as **"within-tier risk ordering underdetermined by the documented policy"** and request the bank's basis for the price it charges in those tiers — e.g. "your documented policy admits refinements that disagree this much about the income-effect for low-income borrowers in tier X; on what basis is the price for tier X set?"
4. Do **not** use any of this to second-guess individual loan decisions (§B.4).

The artifact a bank's model-risk committee would actually see: the *small* set of diverse admissible refinements (3–5 of them, selected to span the band), each with its decision tree shown, plus the tier-level disagreement statistic and the one-sentence "the policy underdetermines feature F for population P in this tier" summary.

### B.6 Limits and the open question

- **What was tested:** LendingClub 36-month consumer loans, a deliberately thin demo policy; bands built on one quarter, the disagreement-geometry and the per-tier-feature pattern confirmed on a second quarter. The qualitative findings (plural-where-the-residual-is-multi-factor; disagreement legible, tail-concentrated, tier-specific; not a case-level signal) are expected to hold more broadly; the magnitudes are LendingClub-specific.
- **The open question (and the next test):** the bands tested here are built on the policy's *named* features only. So this analysis cannot yet answer the sharper question — *do the admissible refinements disagree about structure the documented policy fails to name* (recent credit inquiries, revolving-credit utilization, mortgage-account history, etc.)? If they do, that is a different and **stronger** supervisory signal: not "the policy underdetermines the within-tier risk ordering" but **"the pricing is tracking a feature the documented policy doesn't acknowledge"** — which is *fair-lending adjacent* in a way the current findings are not quite (ECOA/Reg-B disparate-impact territory: a price moving on an unacknowledged feature is the kind of thing that, if that feature proxies a protected characteristic, becomes a disparate-impact question — and even if it doesn't, "undisclosed pricing input" is itself a supervisory concern). It is directly testable with a band construction that admits the extension features into the candidate set and then asks whether the named-feature approximation of the disagreement breaks down (named-explainer R² on `d` drops; the extension features pick up the slack). That test is the immediate next step; its result belongs in this subsection, and if it is positive it materially raises this section's stakes (and reconnects to the protected-class-proxy follow-up already on the menu).

### B.7 One-paragraph summary (for the executive summary up front)

*Within-Rashomon disagreement is a legible, low-dimensional, tier-specific function of the documented policy's features: it tells you, tier by tier, whether the policy pins down the within-tier risk ordering and — when it doesn't — which feature the policy leaves underdetermined and for which borrowers (e.g. "the income-effect for low-income borrowers in tier B1"). That is a real, auditable fact about a tier — in a **policy-risk register**, distinct from the model-risk frame post-hoc attribution lives in — surfaced without any black-box step and at no cost to predictive accuracy, and it is something post-hoc attribution methods structurally cannot produce. It is a tier-level surveillance signal — not a case-level triage signal: routing individual decisions on it was tested and rejected, and the legible tier-specific signal that survived (which feature, which population, in one sentence) is in fact more useful to an examiner than per-case routing would have been, because it needs no per-case inference. The stronger version of the signal — whether the admissible refinements disagree about features the policy never names, which would be fair-lending adjacent — is the next thing being tested.*

## §C — How §A.3 (the pricing finding) and §B (the disagreement finding) fit together *(bridge note — for sequencing the regulator doc, and a methodological caveat to keep honest)*

§A.3 and §B are **two questions asked of the same object** — the per-tier policy-admissible refinement band (§A.2) — and the regulator doc should present them in that order, as question and refinement-of-question, not as two separate results:

- **§A.3 — "Does the documented policy pin down within-tier risk?"** Answered (on the LendingClub demo) by a *significance test*: within each tier, is there residual structure — keyed to a documented policy feature — that predicts realized default above chance, after multiple-comparison control? Binary verdict frame: no (the policy is genuinely binding on approve/deny). Pricing verdict frame: on ~30–50% of the 2015 LendingClub tiers, **yes** — there is robust within-tier residual structure, most often debt-to-income. So the *price* a tier carries is, in those tiers, not determined by the documented policy alone; there is more risk-relevant variation inside the tier than the tier's price reflects.

- **§B — "Where the policy doesn't pin it down, do the policy-*admissible* ways to model that residual agree with each other?"** Answered by the *Rashomon construction*: build the band of policy-admissible refinement models for that tier and see whether they *disagree* about who's higher-risk inside it. On the tiers where the residual is multi-factor, they do (rank-correlation as low as ~0.17 on grade B1), and — §B.2 — the disagreement is a legible, tail-concentrated, **tier-specific** function of one policy feature (income on B1, DTI/FICO elsewhere). §B is the *finer* question: §A.3 establishes the policy underdetermines the price; §B says *by how much, in which direction the admissible models split, and over which feature/population.*

**The methodological caveat (do not conflate the two in the prose).** These are *different formal objects* and the regulator doc must not blur them: §A.3's mechanism is **policy-constrained stratification testing** — per-tier, per-feature, per-threshold two-proportion tests against realized default, with false-discovery control; the "split" that defines residual structure is selected by *statistical significance against a null*, not by membership in an ε-band of equivalent models. §B's mechanism is a **policy-constrained Rashomon construction** — the refinement band is an actual ε-band of interpretable models under a predictive loss; the disagreement is a property of *that set*. So: the phrase "policy-constrained Rashomon" attaches cleanly to §B (and to the binary verdict null, which is also a genuine Rashomon construction — a dual ε-band of depth-bounded trees). It attaches to §A.3 only *in spirit* (it is "Rashomon-flavoured" framing — the policy vocabulary scopes the test — but it is not a Rashomon set). Recommended prose discipline: in §A.3 say "statistical stratification keyed to the policy vocabulary shows the price is underdetermined"; in §B say "the policy-admissible refinement *set* for that tier is plural, and here is what its members disagree about." Calling §A.3 a "Rashomon" result would be an overclaim. (There is an open methodological task — "Rashomon-ify the pricing piece": sweep a within-tier refinement-model class, take the ε-band, check that policy-expressible members beat the constant — which would let "policy-constrained Rashomon" attach to the pricing side properly; §B's construction is most of the machinery for it. Until that's done, keep the layers separate.)

**Where the two halves will weave tighter, once the open P4 test (§B.6) lands.** §A.3's standing contribution is *the lens* — a per-tier classification keyed to the *documented policy's own vocabulary* (Cat 1 / Cat 2 / Cat-2-extension), with the binary-vs-pricing diagnostic — not the empirical fact that within-grade heterogeneity exists (old news in the credit literature). The P4 test ("do the admissible refinements disagree about features the policy never *names*?") connects directly back to that: if the disagreement keys on un-named structure, then the *vocabulary itself* is the thing the audit surfaces as inadequate — which is the §A.3 "the lens is the contribution" point and the §B "tier-level surveillance" point converging on one finding ("the documented policy's vocabulary is too thin to express the risk structure its own admissible refinements are fighting over"). That convergence, if the P4 test supports it, is the regulator doc's strongest single sentence — and the place §A.3, §B, and §A.1 ("what this is") should be cross-referenced.
