## §A.1 — What this is, and what it is not *(drafted section)*

### A.1.1 In one paragraph

This is a method for auditing a lender's *documented* underwriting and pricing policy against the loans it actually made. Given the policy in machine-readable form — the features it names and the directions it asserts ("higher FICO, more creditworthy"; "higher debt-to-income, less") — and loan-level outcomes, the method constructs, for each risk tier the policy defines, the *set of models the policy admits*: shallow, fully-readable decision rules over the policy's named features, obeying the policy's stated monotonicity directions, that are statistically tied at predicting within-tier default. Two things are then read off that set. First, **does it pin the price down** — is there a single within-tier risk ordering all the admissible models agree on, or do they disagree? Second, **when they disagree, over what** — and is that "what" something the policy names, or something it doesn't? The output is a per-tier finding in the policy's own vocabulary: *this tier's price is pinned down*; or *this tier's price is underdetermined, and the admissible refinements split over [a feature the policy names]*; or *this tier's price is underdetermined, and the admissible refinements split over [a feature the policy never names]* — that last being the case where the documented policy a bank hands its examiner is demonstrably not the operative one.

### A.1.2 What it is not

- **Not a black-box explainer.** Post-hoc attribution methods (SHAP, LIME, integrated gradients) take a *trained model* and decompose one of its *outputs* into per-feature contributions, via a surrogate or a sampling scheme that approximates the model. This method never touches the bank's model. It works from the *policy* — and the artifacts it produces (the admissible decision rules) *are* the explanation: a depth-three tree over four named features is its own account of itself; there is no surrogate, no fidelity gap, no "the explanation is approximately faithful to the model" caveat, because there is no model being explained.
- **Not a substitute for the bank's model validation.** SR 11-7 model-risk management — does the bank's model perform, is it stable, is it monitored, is it conceptually sound — is a separate and necessary exercise this does not replace or duplicate. This audits the *stated policy*, which the model is supposed to implement; a bank can have a well-validated model and a documented policy that doesn't describe what that model does, and *that* gap is what this surfaces.
- **Not a case-level triage tool.** It does not say "route this loan to a human." Whether the borrowers the admissible refinements most disagree about are the ones a decision system should hold for review was tested directly, across six configurations, on the most favourable tiers — and rejected (§B.4): per-borrower disagreement tracks where there is residual signal to model, not where the consensus is unreliable. The signal is tier-level surveillance, full stop.
- **Not an enforcement standard.** It is an audit *lens* — a way to see a specific class of gap between a documented policy and the lending it governs. What an examiner does with a flagged tier is ask the bank to account for it (§B.5); the lens does not by itself establish a violation.

### A.1.3 The institutional question — what *kind* of document is this?

The downstream sections say things like "ask the bank to justify the residual structure the price implies in tier X" (§B.5). That instruction only makes sense from a standing that *can* ask the bank for its basis and *can* expect an answer. That requirement — **standing to compel an account** — is the discriminator among the candidate framings, and it sorts them cleanly:

- **Has the standing:** (1) a **supervisory examination work-product** — an examiner runs the construction on the bank's documented policy and outcomes and writes up the flagged tiers as exam findings the bank must respond to; (2) a **third-party audit report** — a firm engaged by the bank's board or audit committee (the Olorin-style engagement) runs the same construction and reports to the engaging body, which has the internal standing to require management's account. These two are *the same artifact in two hands* — the construction, the per-tier findings, the "account for this" ask are identical; only the reporting line differs (to the examiner vs. to the board). **This document is written for that artifact** — primary voice: a third-party audit report whose findings an examiner would also recognize and could re-run. Wherever the text says "the examiner asks…", read "the examining or auditing party, which has standing to ask…".
- **Lacks the standing (so the same content reads differently):** (3) a **bank's voluntary self-disclosure to its examiner** — same construction, but now the bank is volunteering "here are the tiers our documented policy underdetermines, and here is our basis for the price anyway"; the findings become *pre-empted questions the bank answers unprompted* rather than questions put to it. Legitimate and useful, but a different speech act — the document would be framed as "what a bank should disclose, and why disclosing it is in its interest" rather than "what an auditor finds." (4) a **methodology white paper for the field** — the construction described in the abstract, with the LendingClub demo as worked illustration, no specific bank, no "ask"; this is the research-paper register ([[project_three_deliverables]]'s journal/conference pieces), not this document.

The choice is not cosmetic: in framings (1)/(2) the load-bearing verb is *find and require an account*; in (3) it is *disclose and pre-justify*; in (4) it is *demonstrate the method exists and works*. This document commits to (1)/(2) and notes the (3) reading where a sentence would change. *(Pre-registration note for the drafting record: this institutional choice was, as predicted ~30%-likely, partly forced by writing §B.5 out rather than freely picked — "standing to compel an account" is what collapses four candidates into two, and the skeleton's "probably the audit or examiner framing" understated how tightly §B.5 constrains it. The forcing is mild — the skeleton's guess was right — but it is a forcing, not a preference.)*

### A.1.4 The register question — this is policy-risk surveillance, not model-risk management

There is a second framing question, and getting it wrong is the most likely way a supervisor mis-files this method. **It does not live in the SR 11-7 model-risk register.** That register governs the bank's *models* — their performance, stability, conceptual soundness, monitoring; it is where SHAP/LIME and the rest of the model-explainability toolkit sit, because they are tools for inspecting and validating *a model*. This method governs the bank's *stated policy* — whether the policy, taken as written, determines the prices the bank charges, and where it doesn't. That is a **policy-risk** question, and it is not merely a different topic within the same register — it *cannot be formulated* in the model-risk frame. There is no "set of models the policy admits" in a model-risk analysis (there is one model, the bank's, and the question is whether it's good); there is no notion of the documented policy *underdetermining* anything (the policy isn't the object of analysis); the central reading here — "the admissible refinements of this tier disagree, and over a feature the policy doesn't name" — has no expression in a frame whose only object is the bank's trained model and its outputs. So: when a supervisor receives this document, the correct shelf is *policy-consistency / policy-risk* — adjacent to fair-lending policy review and to the question of whether a bank's written underwriting standards describe its actual underwriting — *not* the model-validation shelf. §B.3 and §C lean on this distinction; §A.1 establishes it.

### A.1.5 Why this gap is worth a supervisor's attention (one paragraph, for the executive summary)

A lender's documented policy is, among other things, a *commitment* — the version of its lending behaviour it is willing to state, to its regulator, its borrowers, and its own board. The point of having it written down is that it can be checked. This method checks one specific thing: whether the written commitment is *complete enough to determine the prices the lender charges*. When it isn't — when the policy admits materially different refinements of a tier, or when those refinements split over a feature the policy never names — the operative pricing is being set somewhere the documented commitment doesn't reach, and the gap is exactly the kind of place where drift, undisclosed pricing inputs, and disparate-impact exposure accumulate unobserved (§B.6). Surfacing it is cheap (the construction needs only the documented policy and loan outcomes — no access to the bank's model), produces a finding in the policy's own language (so the bank can act on it directly — tighten the vocabulary, or document the basis), and costs nothing in predictive accuracy ([[project_rashomon_refinement_result]] — the policy-constrained admissible set predicts default no worse than an unconstrained alternative on every tier tested). It is, in short, a way of re-grounding a piece of supervision in the *written commitment* rather than in the black box — which is closer to what underwriting standards were for in the first place.

## §B — Within-Rashomon disagreement as a supervisory signal *(drafted section)*

### B.1 What the disagreement is

When a tier's policy-admissible refinement set (§A.2) contains more than one materially different model, those models *disagree* about which borrowers inside the tier are higher-risk. On the LendingClub 2015 vintages, this happens on the tiers where the within-tier residual is multi-factor: e.g. in one mid-prime tier (grade B1), the admissible refinements rank the tier's borrowers with a pairwise rank-correlation as low as ~0.17 — i.e. policy-admissible refinements of that tier *strongly disagree* about the within-tier risk ordering. On tiers where the residual is single-factor, the set collapses — every "member" is essentially the same model in different feature-subset clothing, and there is nothing to adjudicate. So the first thing this signal reports is binary-per-tier: **does the documented policy admit refinements that disagree about within-tier risk, or not?**

### B.2 What the refinements disagree *about* — and it is legible, and tier-specific

Where the set is plural, the disagreement is not a black-box quantity. For each tier, the per-borrower disagreement is well-approximated by a *three-question decision tree* over one or two of the policy's named features (out-of-sample, the approximation captures roughly half to four-fifths of the variation in the disagreement) — so an examiner can be told, in one sentence, *which* feature the admissible refinements are fighting over and *where*. Two specifics worth stating plainly:

- **The contested feature is different in different tiers.** In the LendingClub 2015 data: in two of the plural tiers the refinements disagree over *debt-to-income*; in two others over *income*; in one over *FICO*. Notably, in the most-disagreeing tier (B1) the contested feature is **income** — the admissible refinements disagree most about the *lowest-income* borrowers within that tier. So "the refinements disagree" is **not one portfolio-level phenomenon** — it is a per-tier fact about which residual feature that tier's admissible refinements happen to carve differently. Report it tier by tier ("in tier X the policy underdetermines the income-effect for low-income borrowers"), never aggregated.
- **The disagreement concentrates at the extremes.** Within the contested feature, the refinements disagree most about borrowers at the *tails* (very high DTI, or very low income, etc.) and least about borrowers in the middle — consistent with the refinements agreeing on the tier's base rate for typical borrowers and only diverging at the edges of the feature range.

### B.3 What this is good for in supervision — two aggregate, tier-by-tier signals

The supervisory value comes in two forms, both *tier-level* (not case-level — see §B.4), both falling out of the policy being *documented and first-class*, and both of a kind a post-hoc attribution method structurally cannot surface (there is no "set of admissible models" for SHAP to disagree, no notion of the policy underdetermining or failing to name anything — SHAP attributes a single model's output, full stop):

- **(i) Within-vocabulary underdetermination — "does the documented policy pin down the within-tier risk ordering?"** When a tier's policy-admissible refinements disagree substantially *and that disagreement is still well-described by the policy's own named features* (the prime tiers in the LendingClub data — A, B), the documented policy is *internally* underdetermined: it names the right features but doesn't constrain how they combine within the tier, so the price the bank charges there is, within the policy's own stated vocabulary, not pinned down. The examiner asks the bank to show the basis for the price (see §B.5).

- **(ii) Vocabulary inadequacy — "are the admissible refinements fighting over features the documented policy never names?"** This is the *stronger* signal, and it was tested directly (see §B.6): on the subprime tiers in the LendingClub data (sub-grades C2, C5, D4 on the 2015 vintage), when the refinement band is allowed the full underwriting feature set, the per-borrower disagreement *stops* being a legible function of the policy's four named features and is carried instead by features the documented policy is silent about — recent credit inquiries, revolving-credit utilization, revolving balance, mortgage-account history. There the finding is not "the policy underdetermines the price within its vocabulary" but **"the price is being set on inputs the documented policy doesn't acknowledge"** — a *vocabulary*-adequacy failure, not just an internal-consistency one. A supervisor should know this: the documented policy a bank hands its examiner is, on those tiers, not the operative one. (This is fair-lending adjacent — §B.6 — because a pricing input the policy doesn't disclose is exactly the kind of thing that, if it proxies a protected characteristic, becomes a disparate-impact question, and even if it doesn't, an undisclosed pricing input is itself a supervisory concern.)

In both forms the use is the same shape: focus examiner attention on the tiers the signal flags, and ask the bank to account for what the signal says is underdetermined or unnamed. It is *aggregate, tier-by-tier* — never a per-borrower verdict (§B.4).

### B.4 What this is **not** good for — case-level triage *(the tested-and-rejected use; state it explicitly)*

It is natural to suppose that the *borrowers* the admissible refinements most disagree about are the borrowers a decision system should route to a human reviewer rather than auto-decide. **This was tested directly, repeatedly, and on the most favourable configuration — on held-out quarters, on the tiers where the disagreement is real, including the subprime tiers where the documented policy is *blind to the contested feature* (the case where routing should work if it works anywhere) — and it does not hold.** The high-disagreement borrowers are *not* the ones where the consensus prediction is least reliable; if anything they are slightly *more* predictable and better-calibrated. The reason (confirmed by the geometry result, §B.2): per-borrower disagreement tracks *where the within-tier residual signal is*, not *where the models are confused* — the refinements diverge where there is structure to carve differently, which tends to be where there is *more* to predict, not less. The rejection is robust across the variants tried — the named-feature band, the full-underwriting-feature band under two different definitions of "distinct refinement", three different ways of slicing the disagreement (raw, residual-after-removing-the-policy-explainable-part, policy-explainable-part), and a direct policy-blind-half-vs-policy-named-half comparison within the highest-disagreement borrowers — six configurations, all negative; the few "route here" indicators that flicker on in any of them fire on calibration/error differences of order one part in a thousand on error rates of fifteen to twenty per cent, and flip sign across slices and across tiers. That is noise, not a signal. **Consequently: do not use refinement-disagreement as an auto-decide-vs-route trigger.** It is a *tier-level surveillance* signal, not a *case-level triage* signal — and this is now a settled question, not an open one. (One narrow legitimate case-level use survives: if a decision is *already* under human review for an independent reason — a borrower complaint, an adverse-action appeal, an audit sample — then showing the reviewer the two most-disagreeing admissible refinements and the feature they fight over is a useful way to frame the residual judgement the reviewer faces. But the disagreement does not *choose* the review queue.) We document this as a tested-and-rejected design alternative rather than omitting it, because the temptation to use it that way is real and the rejection is informative — and because a regulator reading "the admissible refinements disagree about who's risky inside this tier" will reach for exactly this inference, and should be told it was checked.

### B.5 What an examiner does with it, concretely

Given a bank's documented underwriting/pricing policy (in machine-readable form) and loan-level outcomes:

1. For each risk tier, construct the policy-admissible refinement band (§A.2).
2. Report, per tier: whether the band is plural; if so, a disagreement magnitude (the worst pairwise rank-correlation among admissible refinements), the contested feature, and the region of that feature where the disagreement concentrates.
3. Flag the tiers with large disagreement as **"within-tier risk ordering underdetermined by the documented policy"** and request the bank's basis for the price it charges in those tiers — e.g. "your documented policy admits refinements that disagree this much about the income-effect for low-income borrowers in tier X; on what basis is the price for tier X set?"
4. Do **not** use any of this to second-guess individual loan decisions (§B.4).

The artifact a bank's model-risk committee would actually see: the *small* set of diverse admissible refinements (3–5 of them, selected to span the band), each with its decision tree shown, plus the tier-level disagreement statistic and the one-sentence "the policy underdetermines feature F for population P in this tier" summary.

### B.6 Limits, what was tested, and the vocabulary-adequacy finding

- **What was tested:** LendingClub 36-month consumer loans, a deliberately thin demo policy (four named features); bands built on one quarter, the disagreement-geometry, the per-tier-feature pattern, the vocabulary-adequacy finding, and the no-case-level-routing result all confirmed on held-out quarters. The qualitative findings (plural-where-the-residual-is-multi-factor; disagreement legible, tail-concentrated, tier-specific; vocabulary-adequacy quality-stratified; not a case-level signal) are expected to hold more broadly; the magnitudes are LendingClub-specific.

- **The vocabulary-adequacy finding (this was the open question; it is now answered).** The bands described in §B.1–B.2 are built on the policy's *named* features only, so by construction the disagreement there is a function of those features. We then rebuilt the bands with the *full underwriting feature set* admitted into the candidate models (recent credit inquiries, revolving-credit utilization, revolving balance, mortgage-account history, and the rest — features the documented policy is silent about; the policy's stated monotonicity directions still enforced on the named features, the rest unconstrained) and asked whether the disagreement stays legible in the four named features or moves onto the unnamed ones. The answer is **quality-stratified**: on the prime tiers (sub-grades A1, A5, B1) and on sub-grade C1, the per-borrower disagreement is still well-approximated by a short tree over the four named features (roughly 40–66% of its variation recovered) — these tiers are *internally* underdetermined (§B.3(i)) but the policy's vocabulary is adequate to describe the indeterminacy. On sub-grades C2, C5, and D4, the named-feature approximation collapses (≈ 6–25% of the variation recovered) and the full-feature approximation recovers it (≈ 51–57%) — the disagreement there is carried by the *unnamed* features. So on three of the four subprime sub-grades on this vintage, **the price is being set on inputs the documented policy doesn't acknowledge** (§B.3(ii)) — a stronger and different supervisory signal than internal underdetermination. (Methodological note for honesty: which tiers land in which bucket depends on how one counts "distinct refinements" — counting by *which features a model uses* rather than by exact tree shape moves sub-grade C1 from the collapsed bucket into the legible one, because much of its apparent collapse was variation among near-identical trees at slightly different thresholds, which the named features can't track. Counting by feature set is the more policy-meaningful definition and the one used for the split above. The honest summary is "subprime-skewed — three of the four subprime sub-grades on this vintage", not "the subprime tiers exactly".)

- **Fair-lending bite, stated carefully.** The tiers where the documented policy is silent about the contested feature are *subprime-skewed*, and subprime borrower pools skew toward protected classes through well-documented mechanisms — so "the documented policy is silent about the feature the admissible refinements fight over, and that silence concentrates in the subprime tiers" is a specific fair-lending flag (ECOA/Reg-B disparate-impact territory: a price moving on an unacknowledged feature is the kind of thing that becomes a disparate-impact question if that feature proxies a protected characteristic — and an undisclosed pricing input is a supervisory concern even if it doesn't). Two cautions on the strength of this: (1) it is *subprime-skewed, not the subprime tiers exactly* (sub-grade C1 is the counter-example) — don't overstate the alignment; (2) the next test — does the population on which the policy's vocabulary is inadequate cluster by a protected-class proxy (geography, age-from-credit-history-length, etc.)? — is a *live follow-up*, not done. (That follow-up is now in the *observability* direction only: there is no "route the affected borrowers" angle, because — §B.4 — there is no routable population; the question is purely "is the documented policy's vocabulary inadequate *more* for some protected groups than others?". A cleaner substrate for it is HMDA mortgage data, which carries reported race/ethnicity/sex directly with no proxy step.)

- **The case-level-routing question is closed.** Not an open beat — see §B.4. It was tested across six configurations and rejected; the regulator doc should present it as a tested-and-rejected alternative, not a loose end.

### B.7 One-paragraph summary (for the executive summary up front)

*Within-Rashomon disagreement is a legible, low-dimensional, tier-specific quantity that tells you, tier by tier, whether the documented policy pins down the within-tier risk ordering and — when it doesn't — what it leaves underdetermined. On the prime tiers (and on one subprime sub-grade) the disagreement is well-described by the policy's own named features, and the finding there is internal: the policy names the right features but doesn't constrain how they combine within the tier, so the price it implies is, in its own vocabulary, not pinned down — e.g. "the income-effect for low-income borrowers in tier B1". On three of the four subprime sub-grades on the LendingClub 2015 vintage the disagreement instead keys on features the documented policy never names (recent credit inquiries, revolving-credit utilization, revolving balance, mortgage-account history), and the finding there is stronger: the price is being set on inputs the documented policy doesn't acknowledge — a vocabulary-adequacy failure that is fair-lending adjacent because the affected tiers skew subprime, and a tier-quality-stratified diagnostic of where the documented policy a bank hands its examiner is not the operative one. All of this is a real, auditable fact about a tier — in a **policy-risk register**, distinct from the model-risk frame post-hoc attribution lives in — surfaced without any black-box step and at no cost to predictive accuracy, and it is something post-hoc attribution methods structurally cannot produce. It is a tier-level surveillance signal — not a case-level triage signal: routing individual decisions on it was tested across six configurations and rejected (per-borrower disagreement tracks where there is residual signal to model, not where the consensus is unreliable), and the legible tier-specific signal that survived is in fact more useful to an examiner than per-case routing would have been, because it needs no per-case inference.*

## §C — How §A.3 (the pricing finding) and §B (the disagreement finding) fit together *(bridge note — for sequencing the regulator doc, and a methodological caveat to keep honest)*

§A.3 and §B are **two questions asked of the same object** — the per-tier policy-admissible refinement band (§A.2) — and the regulator doc should present them in that order, as question and refinement-of-question, not as two separate results:

- **§A.3 — "Does the documented policy pin down within-tier risk?"** Answered (on the LendingClub demo) by a *significance test*: within each tier, is there residual structure — keyed to a documented policy feature — that predicts realized default above chance, after multiple-comparison control? Binary verdict frame: no (the policy is genuinely binding on approve/deny). Pricing verdict frame: on ~30–50% of the 2015 LendingClub tiers, **yes** — there is robust within-tier residual structure, most often debt-to-income. So the *price* a tier carries is, in those tiers, not determined by the documented policy alone; there is more risk-relevant variation inside the tier than the tier's price reflects.

- **§B — "Where the policy doesn't pin it down, do the policy-*admissible* ways to model that residual agree with each other?"** Answered by the *Rashomon construction*: build the band of policy-admissible refinement models for that tier and see whether they *disagree* about who's higher-risk inside it. On the tiers where the residual is multi-factor, they do (rank-correlation as low as ~0.17 on grade B1), and — §B.2 — the disagreement is a legible, tail-concentrated, **tier-specific** function of one policy feature (income on B1, DTI/FICO elsewhere). §B is the *finer* question: §A.3 establishes the policy underdetermines the price; §B says *by how much, in which direction the admissible models split, and over which feature/population.*

**The methodological caveat (do not conflate the two in the prose).** These are *different formal objects* and the regulator doc must not blur them: §A.3's mechanism is **policy-constrained stratification testing** — per-tier, per-feature, per-threshold two-proportion tests against realized default, with false-discovery control; the "split" that defines residual structure is selected by *statistical significance against a null*, not by membership in an ε-band of equivalent models. §B's mechanism is a **policy-constrained Rashomon construction** — the refinement band is an actual ε-band of interpretable models under a predictive loss; the disagreement is a property of *that set*. So: the phrase "policy-constrained Rashomon" attaches cleanly to §B (and to the binary verdict null, which is also a genuine Rashomon construction — a dual ε-band of depth-bounded trees). It attaches to §A.3 only *in spirit* (it is "Rashomon-flavoured" framing — the policy vocabulary scopes the test — but it is not a Rashomon set). Recommended prose discipline: in §A.3 say "statistical stratification keyed to the policy vocabulary shows the price is underdetermined"; in §B say "the policy-admissible refinement *set* for that tier is plural, and here is what its members disagree about." Calling §A.3 a "Rashomon" result would be an overclaim. (There is an open methodological task — "Rashomon-ify the pricing piece": sweep a within-tier refinement-model class, take the ε-band, check that policy-expressible members beat the constant — which would let "policy-constrained Rashomon" attach to the pricing side properly; §B's construction is most of the machinery for it. Until that's done, keep the layers separate.)

**How the two halves converge — the regulator doc's load-bearing sentence (the P4 test has now landed and supports it).** §A.3's standing contribution is *the lens* — a per-tier classification keyed to the *documented policy's own vocabulary* (Cat 1 / Cat 2 / Cat-2-extension), with the binary-vs-pricing diagnostic — not the empirical fact that within-grade heterogeneity exists (old news in the credit literature). §B's tested result connects directly back to that. Read them together and they are two readings of one fact — *the documented policy is not the operative one* — at different removes:

- **§A.3 (pricing):** there is risk-relevant structure inside the tier that the documented policy *names* (debt-to-income, income — features in its vocabulary) but *does not act on* — the price doesn't move with it. The documented policy is incomplete *within* its own vocabulary.
- **§B (disagreement, prime tiers + C1):** the policy-admissible refinements of those tiers *disagree* about how the named features combine — the documented policy doesn't even pin down the *direction* of the within-tier ordering its own vocabulary could express. Still inside the vocabulary, one further remove of incompleteness.
- **§B (disagreement, subprime sub-grades C2/C5/D4):** the policy-admissible refinements there disagree about features the documented policy *never names at all* — the indeterminacy has moved *outside* the vocabulary; the operative pricing is reaching for inputs the documented policy doesn't acknowledge. The vocabulary *itself* is the thing the audit surfaces as inadequate.

So the regulator doc's strongest single sentence is roughly: *the documented policy a bank hands its examiner is, on the tiers this audit flags, not the operative one — sometimes because the price ignores structure the policy names, sometimes because the policy underdetermines how its own named features combine, and on the subprime tiers because the operative price is reaching for features the policy never disclosed* — and that is the place §A.3, §B, and §A.1 ("what this is") should be cross-referenced. Note the convergence is *quality-stratified*, not uniform — say so: the "vocabulary itself is inadequate" reading is the subprime-skewed C2/C5/D4 result, not a portfolio fact (§B.6's nuance).
