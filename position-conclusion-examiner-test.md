# Conclusion (EXAMINER-PRIMARY REGISTER — TEST DRAFT)

The Financial Services AI Risk Management Framework arrived in February 2026 with 230 control objectives and no interpretive frame for what they collectively protect. Read as a checklist, the framework is operationally daunting and conceptually opaque. Read as a structure, it has an underlying coherence — empty-chair representation — that makes both interpretation and operationalization tractable, and that gives examiners diagnostic leverage the checklist does not provide.

We have argued for three positions in this paper, each of which constrains the others.

The first is that examiners reviewing AI deployments under the FS AI RMF require an interpretive frame the framework does not supply. Without such a frame, 230 control objectives collapse into compliance theater: institutions demonstrate adherence to controls without demonstrating the underlying integrity the controls were written to ensure, and examiners reviewing such demonstrations have no methodology for distinguishing substantive representation from documentary performance. The empty-chair frame supplies the missing methodology. Examiners ask which absent parties each control was meant to protect, which the institution's implementation actually represents, and what the gap between the two reveals about the institution's posture. Controls whose implementations represent the empty chairs they were meant to protect have substance; controls whose implementations do not are decoration. The distinction is not stylistic. It is the difference between examination that produces accurate findings and examination that documents institutions' compliance claims.

The second is that examiners cannot accept post-hoc explanation as a verification regime when reviewing AI deployments in adversarial regulatory contexts, and bank examination is structurally adversarial. An adverse action notice generated by a Shapley-value attribution applied to an underwriting model's denial decision is an artifact whose form satisfies regulatory text without binding to the model's reasoning. An examiner who accepts the artifact as evidence of substantive empty-chair representation has accepted form for substance, and the examination's findings will mistake compliance theater for compliance. The categorical the examiner must hold is that explanation produced by a process separate from the model's reasoning does not constitute verification, regardless of the explanation's surface plausibility or the institution's documentation regime around it. Examination practice that accepts post-hoc explanation as verification produces findings that will not survive scrutiny when later examination practice learns to ask what an honest verification regime requires. The frame's second contribution is giving examiners the categorical to hold now, before the regulatory community has fully developed it.

The third is that examiners cannot accept human signature on AI-generated decisions as evidence that human oversight has been performed, when the human's review was of the explanation rather than the underlying reasoning. A reviewer who reads an adverse action notice and signs it has verified that the notice is well-formed; the reviewer has not verified that the reasons given are the reasons the model used, because the reasons the model used are not present in the artifact reviewed. The signature attests to review of the explanation. The decision was made by the AI. The reasoning was never verified. The empty chair has been moved further from the room, not closer to it. Examiners who accept this configuration as adequate human oversight are accepting the org-chart picture of accountability for the substantive picture, and the substantive picture shows an empty accountability locus. The frame's third contribution is making this configuration visible as the dishonest middle the examiner is seeing, rather than as the human-in-the-loop architecture the institution is claiming.

These positions narrow the scope of what examiners can find adequate but do not foreclose AI in community banking. AI applications in which a verification regime is structurally available — pattern recognition surfacing cases for human investigation, anomaly detection flagging unusual patterns, consistency monitoring identifying decisions that deviate from institutional norms — remain reviewable on their own terms, and examination practice for these applications has tractable methodology. The human reasons through the surfaced case with full context; the reasoning becomes the institution's audit trail; the audit trail can be examined by ordinary means. AI applications in which a verification regime is not structurally available — decision generation by models whose outputs are communicated through post-hoc explanation methods, with human signatures attesting to review of the explanation rather than verification of the reasoning — fall on the wrong side of a structural line that no current architecture has crossed. Examiners reviewing such applications can document the structural inadequacy, but cannot find the applications adequate, regardless of how fully the institution's compliance documentation satisfies the FS AI RMF's text. The distinction is not a matter of risk tolerance. It is a matter of whether absent parties are represented or not, and the examiner's institutional role is precisely to enforce that distinction.

The diagnostic techniques developed in this paper — empty-chair enumeration, pages missing from the ledger, the produced-silence question pair — operationalize the frame for examiners. Their adoption does not require regulatory amendment; the FS AI RMF's existing text supports their application as interpretive methodology, because the text's underlying coherence is the empty-chair frame the techniques operationalize. Examiners can apply these techniques within current authority. Institutions encountering examination conducted with these techniques will discover that compliance documentation prepared for the checklist surface does not produce the evidence the techniques require, and will adjust their architectural choices accordingly. The frame's regulatory effect propagates from examination practice to architectural practice, with the examination practice as the leverage point.

The architectural primitives that empty-chair representation requires — tamper-evident temporal capture, evidence binding, three-dimensional confidence characterization, structural drift typology, diagnostic instrumentation — are sketched in this paper at the property level and left to follow-on work for implementation specification. Our concern here has been with the frame and its examination-methodology operationalization, not with the build. But the frame's value is in the examination posture it forces honest implementations toward, not in any claim that current implementations have arrived. Community banks deploying AI under the FS AI RMF face a genuine question about what *commensurate* compliance means for their context. The frame we have proposed provides the interpretive substrate examiners can use to answer it, and the answer institutions will receive in examination will shape what they build next.

The empty chairs are watching, and so are the examiners learning to see them. The paper's claim is that examination practice can hold the line on substantive empty-chair representation now, before the architectural primitives that would close the structural gaps are fully developed, by giving examiners the frame and the techniques to recognize the dishonest middle when they encounter it. The frame is the methodology; the techniques are the practice; the examiner is the agent whose work makes the frame's claims load-bearing in the regulatory environment. Whether community banking AI matures toward substantive empty-chair representation or sediments around the dishonest middle depends substantially on whether examiners adopt the frame and the techniques in the period before the architectural primitives are widely available.
