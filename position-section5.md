The empty-chair frame produces interpretive structure (Section 2), a structural taxonomy of AI applications (Section 3), and diagnostic techniques that operationalize the frame as design discipline and examination methodology (Section 4). This section names the architectural primitive categories that empty-chair representation requires. The treatment is at the property level: each primitive is described by what it must do, not by how it must be implemented. Implementation specification is the work of follow-on papers, and we mark places where the discussion is at risk of crossing into implementation territory so that the property-level claims remain the substance of this section.

A framing note before the primitives are developed. The four primitives below are *silence-breaking instruments*, not improvements to existing metrics. Read through a Goodhart lens — "the controls became targets, so we need better controls" — they appear underwhelming, because Goodhart-shaped readings expect the fix to be at the metric layer. The frame's diagnosis (Section 4) is that the failure mode is silence-manufacture: the architecture suppresses access to ground truth and produces a substitute artifact that occupies the rhetorical position evidence should occupy. The fix has to live at the silence layer — making the suppressed ground truth contemporaneously inspectable, traversable, and characterized honestly. Each primitive corresponds to a specific class of silence the architecture currently produces, and the primitive's value is in breaking it. We mark this here because readers approaching the section without the frame's diagnostic structure are likely to mis-read the primitives as competing implementations of better controls, and the mis-reading produces frustrated assessments of adequacy that the structural argument does not warrant.

The primitives are not exhaustive. They are the categories the diagnostic techniques in Section 4 require. An honest AI governance system for community banking will likely require additional primitives we have not identified; the four named here are the categories we can argue for from the frame's structural commitments alone.

## Primitive 1: Tamper-evident temporal capture

The property: the architecture captures decision context at the moment the decision is made, in a form that binds the captured context to the moment of capture, that prevents retroactive alteration of either the context or the temporal binding, and that can be later inspected without reliance on the institution's reconstruction of what was captured.

The diagnostic technique that requires this primitive: pages missing from the ledger (Section 4, Technique 2). The technique reads patterns of presence and absence in attested decisions as substantive information about the institution's posture. The reading requires that present attestations be trustworthy — that an attestation present in the record was actually produced at the time it claims, contains what it claims to contain, and has not been altered since. Without these guarantees, present attestations are no more diagnostic than absent ones; the institution can produce attestations after the fact that resemble contemporaneous capture.

The property has three components. First, *contemporaneous capture*: the attestation is produced as the decision is made, not reconstructed afterward. Second, *temporal binding*: the attestation's claim to a particular time of capture is itself attested in a way that resists later alteration. Third, *content binding*: what the attestation says was captured cannot be changed after capture without the change being detectable.

The empty chair this primitive most directly serves is the institution's future self. An examiner arriving in three years to review decisions made now can rely on contemporaneous attestation in a way they cannot rely on reconstructed narrative; the future institution's exposure to enforcement risk from gradually-degraded documentation is structurally addressed by capture that cannot be retroactively improved.

The primitive also serves the future compliance officer (institutional knowledge survives personnel turnover in a form that does not depend on the departing officer's narrative reconstruction) and indirectly serves the customer affected by an AI decision (the customer's challenge to a decision made years prior can rely on what the institution attested at the time of decision, rather than on what the institution claims now to remember).

[QUARRY: PAPER 2] Implementation choices for tamper-evident temporal capture include cryptographic anchoring to public timestamp services, append-only event-sourced storage with cryptographic chaining, distributed attestation across nodes with byzantine fault tolerance, and various combinations of these. Different implementations have different cost profiles, different examination-time inspection mechanisms, and different exposure to specific attack classes. Paper 2 specifies the implementation tradeoffs at the level of architectural commitment.

The property-level claim is that any honest implementation must satisfy contemporaneous capture, temporal binding, and content binding. Implementations that satisfy two of three are not partial successes; they are failures in the dimension they fail. An attestation regime with content binding and temporal binding but without contemporaneous capture allows reconstructed narrative to be cryptographically signed; the signature attests to the institution's commitment to the narrative, not to the contemporaneous accuracy of the captured decision context. This is a sophisticated form of the dishonest middle that the primitive's three-component definition is meant to foreclose.

## Primitive 2: Evidence binding

The property: the architecture binds decisions to the evidence that supported them, in a way that the binding can be later inspected, traversed, and verified. A decision attested without evidence is not the decision the empty chair needs to inspect; the empty chair needs the decision and the chain of evidence the decision rested on.

The diagnostic technique that requires this primitive: empty-chair enumeration (Section 4, Technique 1). The technique requires that, for each empty chair a control was meant to protect, the institution can demonstrate evidence that the implementation represents that chair. Demonstration requires evidence; evidence requires that the architecture preserved the chain from decision back to supporting material. Without evidence binding, demonstration reduces to assertion.

The property has two components. First, *evidence preservation*: the architecture preserves the material that supported each decision in a form that survives institutional turnover, system migration, and ordinary records management. Second, *traversal capability*: an examiner or other inspector can walk from a decision backward through the evidence that supported it, and forward from a piece of evidence to the decisions that relied on it. The two-direction traversal matters because empty-chair questions arrive in both forms — *what evidence supported this decision* and *what decisions depended on this evidence source*.

The empty chair this primitive most directly serves varies by which decision is being inspected. For an adverse action notice, the empty chair is the denied applicant, and evidence binding allows inspection of what the institution actually relied on (which may or may not match the post-hoc explanation produced for the notice). For a loan-pricing decision, the empty chair is the priced applicant, and evidence binding allows inspection of which underwriting features actually drove the rate. For a SAR filing decision, the empty chair is shared between the next victim of fraud (whose protection depended on the filing) and the customer who was reported (whose interest is in whether the filing was substantively warranted).

[QUARRY: PAPER 2] Implementation choices for evidence binding include graph-structured evidence stores with explicit edges between decisions and supporting material, content-addressed storage where evidence is referenced by cryptographic hash of content, lineage-tracking systems that maintain provenance metadata across transformations, and various hybrid approaches. The implementation choice has substantial implications for query performance, storage cost, and the granularity at which evidence can be traversed. Paper 2 specifies these tradeoffs.

The property-level claim is distinct from logging: a system that logs decisions and logs evidence but does not bind them is not satisfying this primitive. The binding is what enables traversal. A pile of documents and a pile of decisions, both well-attested, are not evidence-bound unless the architecture preserves the relation between them.

A specific structural concern: evidence binding interacts with post-hoc explanation methods in a way the primitive must address explicitly. A system that binds the post-hoc explanation to the decision satisfies the primitive's literal requirement while undermining its substantive purpose. The empty chair inspecting the bound evidence finds a Shapley-value attribution; the attribution was bound to the decision; the binding is verifiable. The primitive is satisfied. The empty chair has not been served. The honest formulation of the primitive requires that the bound evidence be the *underlying reasoning* the decision was based on, not a post-hoc reconstruction. This is why the primitive cannot be specified in isolation from the categoricals from the opening: the primitive's property-level definition must include the categoricals, or the primitive can be implemented in ways that satisfy its form without serving its function.

## Primitive 3: Three-dimensional confidence characterization

The property: the architecture characterizes the epistemic state of every assessment along three dimensions — confidence (evidence supports the conclusion), concern (evidence supports an alternative conclusion), and indeterminacy (evidence is insufficient to determine either) — rather than collapsing epistemic state to a single confidence score.

The diagnostic technique that requires this primitive: empty-chair enumeration applied to representation evidence (Section 4, Technique 1). The technique asks whether the institution has evidence that the implementation represents each empty chair. The honest answer to this question, for many cases, is "we do not know" — the institution's data does not establish that representation occurred, but neither does it establish that representation failed. The honest answer is indeterminacy, not confidence in a particular direction. A confidence-only architecture cannot express indeterminacy; it must report a confidence value, and the confidence value is then read as the institution's belief about the case, when the institution's actual epistemic state was ignorance.

The property has three components corresponding to the three dimensions. *Confidence* measures evidence supporting the conclusion. *Concern* measures evidence supporting alternatives. *Indeterminacy* measures the insufficiency of evidence to determine either. The three are orthogonal: high confidence and high concern simultaneously indicates a contested case where evidence pulls both ways; low confidence and low concern with high indeterminacy indicates a case the institution does not have grounds to evaluate.

The empty chair this primitive serves is the affected party who depends on the institution's epistemic state being honestly characterized. A customer denied credit on a high-confidence assessment can challenge the assessment substantively. A customer denied credit on a high-indeterminacy assessment that was reported as moderate confidence has been deceived about the institution's actual state; the institution's reporting collapsed indeterminacy to a confidence value the institution did not actually have grounds for.

[QUARRY: PAPER 2] Implementation choices for three-dimensional confidence characterization include neutrosophic-set-theoretic representations, evidence-theoretic frameworks (Dempster-Shafer and related), explicit indeterminacy flags layered on conventional confidence scores, and various other approaches. The choice has implications for how the three dimensions are computed, how they are reported in artifacts the empty chair encounters, and how they propagate through chained assessments. Paper 2 specifies these.

The property-level claim is that any honest implementation must distinguish all three dimensions; an implementation that maps everything to a single confidence value is not satisfying this primitive even if the value is computed sophisticatedly. The distinction matters most in the cases where institutions are most tempted to elide it: cases where the institution's actual epistemic state is indeterminacy but where reporting indeterminacy is operationally inconvenient. Those are precisely the cases where the primitive's diagnostic value is highest.

## Primitive 4: Structural drift typology

The property: the architecture monitors and reports drift along distinct types — data drift, concept drift, performance drift, and institutional drift — rather than collapsing drift to a single performance metric.

The diagnostic technique that requires this primitive: produced-silence question pair (Section 4, Technique 3). The technique asks what silences the architecture is producing and what it is inferring from those silences. Drift typology is structurally relevant because different drift types reveal different architectural patterns of silence-production. Performance drift may indicate model degradation; data drift may indicate population shifts the model was not trained for; concept drift may indicate that the relationships the model was trained on have changed; institutional drift may indicate divergence between de facto practice and de jure policy. Each is a different architectural fact about what the institution's deployment is doing, and each has different implications for empty-chair representation.

The property has four components corresponding to the four drift types. *Data drift* tracks distributional changes in inputs. *Concept drift* tracks changes in the relationship between inputs and the phenomena they predict. *Performance drift* tracks changes in the model's outputs against ground truth or proxies. *Institutional drift* tracks divergence between the institution's documented policy and its operational practice as captured in the attestation regime.

The empty chair varies by drift type. Data drift most directly affects the marginalized customer (whose population may have shifted in ways the model does not track). Concept drift affects the institution's future self (whose enforcement exposure depends on whether the model's predictions remain valid). Performance drift affects the next victim of fraud or the false-positive customer (whose exposure depends on the model's continued accuracy). Institutional drift affects the future compliance officer (who needs to understand how practice diverged from policy) and the examiner (whose review requires that documented policy match operational practice).

[QUARRY: PAPER 2] Implementation choices for drift detection include statistical hypothesis testing on input distributions, embedding-space change detection, performance comparison against rolling baselines, and various meta-monitoring approaches that detect drift in the drift detection itself. Paper 2 specifies these.

The property-level claim is that an architecture monitoring "drift" without distinguishing the four types is monitoring a composite signal whose components require different responses. A performance-drift response (recalibration, retraining) is not a data-drift response (population analysis, training-data update) is not an institutional-drift response (policy review, attestation-pattern audit). Conflating them is the architectural equivalent of treating a fever as the underlying disease.

## What this section does not include

The strawman material that informs this section includes a fifth category — institutional self-knowledge — that we have not promoted to a primitive in this section. Our reading is that institutional self-knowledge is not a primitive in the same sense as the four above; it is an *emergent property* of the four primitives operating together over time. An institution with tamper-evident temporal capture, evidence binding, three-dimensional confidence characterization, and structural drift typology accumulates institutional self-knowledge as a byproduct of the four primitives' operation. Naming it as a fifth primitive risks confusing the goal with the mechanism.

This is a structural call we make explicit because a reader familiar with the strawman may expect the fifth primitive and notice its absence. The choice is open to revision; if the four primitives produce institutional self-knowledge less robustly than this section claims, a fifth primitive may need to be specified. Paper 2's implementation work will surface whether the byproduct emerges as claimed.

We have also deliberately not included primitives that would specify the AI components themselves — model architecture choices, training methodology, ante-hoc interpretability techniques, tensor-channel observation mechanisms. These are valuable and the paper's argument leans on some of them (particularly tensor-channel observation as the structural alternative to text-channel verification). But specifying them as primitives in this section would commit the position paper to claims about what AI systems should look like internally, which is a different argument than the architectural-governance argument the paper is making. The AI components are the subject of separate research; the governance architecture is what this paper specifies.

## What the four primitives compound to

The four primitives, operating together, produce an architecture that supports the diagnostic techniques of Section 4 and that serves the empty chairs enumerated across Sections 2 and 3. Tamper-evident temporal capture makes the institution's reasoning inspectable; evidence binding makes the inspection traversable; three-dimensional confidence characterization makes the inspection's findings honest about institutional epistemic state; structural drift typology makes the inspection's findings useful across time as conditions change.

This is the architectural commitment empty-chair representation requires. The commitment is at the property level; multiple coherent implementations of each primitive exist; the choice among implementations is the work of follow-on papers and of the architects who will build community banking AI governance systems under FS AI RMF compliance pressure.

The primitives' substantive contribution is structural rather than methodological. An architecture without these primitives cannot represent the empty chairs the FS AI RMF was written to protect, regardless of how thoroughly the architecture documents its compliance. An architecture with these primitives can represent the empty chairs, even where current regulatory text does not require the representation to be substantive. The primitives are the structural minimum for honest empty-chair representation in AI governance; they are not the maximum, and Paper 2's implementation work will identify additional primitives the implementation reveals as necessary.

The honest uncertainty for this section, picked up in Section 6: we have argued for the four primitives from the structural commitments of the empty-chair frame and the diagnostic techniques the frame produces. We have not demonstrated that an implementation of the four primitives is operationally tractable for a community bank under cost and resource constraints. The frame's claim is structural; the implementation's tractability is empirical, and the empirical question is the work of Paper 2 and of subsequent deployment validation. The primitives' value in this paper is as the structural argument; their viability in practice is a question we do not resolve here.
