# Section 6: Honest Uncertainties

---

A position paper that claims certainty everywhere is not to be trusted. The frame, the categoricals, the diagnostic techniques, and the architectural primitives developed in this paper are advanced as defensible structural commitments, not as solved problems. This section names what the paper has not resolved, what its structural arguments do not establish, and where the load-bearing claims rest on conditions the paper cannot verify. The point is not to weaken the paper's positions but to mark where the positions stop, so that subsequent work — whether by these authors or others — can identify the next questions accurately.

We organize the uncertainties by the level of the paper's argument they affect: the frame itself, the categoricals, the diagnostic techniques, and the architectural primitives. A final uncertainty concerns the paper's overall theory of how a position paper produces effect, which is the meta-level concern that the substantive uncertainties fold into.

## Uncertainties about the frame

**The empty-chair count is open.** The frame names absent parties as the organizing principle but does not specify a closed enumeration of which parties count as empty chairs in any given regulatory context. Section 2's six chairs in BSA/AML monitoring are illustrative, not exhaustive. Different analysts using the frame may identify different chairs, weight them differently, and reach different conclusions about whether a given control's implementation represents the chairs adequately. The frame does not provide a procedure for determining which chairs are present in a given context; it provides a vocabulary and a set of diagnostic questions that, applied conscientiously, surface chairs that would otherwise be implicit.

This is not a fatal indeterminacy. Most useful analytical frames are open in this way; the alternative is a frame so specified that it cannot be applied to novel situations. But the openness is real, and it has consequences. Two examiners using the frame on the same institution may disagree about which chairs the institution is failing to represent. The disagreement may be productive — surfacing different aspects of the institution's posture — or unproductive, depending on whether the examiners can articulate the chairs they are weighting and the reasoning behind the weighting. The frame's value depends on practitioners developing the discipline to make their chair-attribution explicit, which is a craft skill the paper does not specify and that practice will have to develop.

**The structural-versus-produced absence distinction may not be exhaustive.** Section 2 introduced the distinction; subsequent sections relied on it. The distinction is sharp where it applies, but we have not argued that all absence falls into one of the two categories. Hybrid cases — partly structural, partly produced — appear in Section 2's enumeration, and the paper handles them by marking them as mixed. Whether all absence can be analyzed in terms of these two categories, or whether further categories (collective absence, structural-but-architecturally-correctable absence, others) are needed, is an open question the paper does not resolve.

**The frame's generalization beyond community banking is asserted but not demonstrated.** We have argued that the empty-chair frame applies to AI governance in regulated decision contexts generally, with examples drawn from community banking. We have not demonstrated the frame's application to insurance underwriting, to healthcare AI, to criminal justice algorithms, to social-services determinations. The frame's structural commitments suggest it should generalize, but the generalization is a hypothesis the paper does not test. Other regulatory contexts may have empty chairs the banking frame does not anticipate; the diagnostic techniques may need adaptation; the architectural primitives may need supplementation. The paper's claims about generalizability are best read as invitations for sectoral application rather than as established results.

## Uncertainties about the categoricals

**The categoricals are structural arguments, not empirical claims.** Position 2 (post-hoc explanation as inadequate verification regime in adversarial contexts) and Position 3 (human oversight of explanations as not verification of reasoning) are argued from the structural relationship between explanation methods and model reasoning, with citations to empirical work that supports the argument. The categoricals are not themselves empirical claims; they are structural claims that empirical work has corroborated in specific instances. Whether the categoricals hold in their fully general form — across all explanation methods, all model architectures, all adversarial contexts — is a stronger claim than the empirical work has established. We argue for the structural form because the structural argument is what the paper's other claims rest on; we acknowledge that the structural form has not been demonstrated empirically across all the configurations it claims to apply to.

**The "structurally adversarial" characterization of bank examination is a claim about institutional dynamics, not a definitional truth.** Examination practice involves adversarial elements (the examiner is testing institutional claims) and cooperative elements (the examiner and institution share an interest in the institution's continued operation under regulatory authority). The paper characterizes examination as structurally adversarial because the verification-regime question requires the adversarial framing to land, but a more nuanced reading would distinguish adversarial examination contexts (where the institution and examiner have genuinely opposed interests on a specific question) from cooperative examination contexts (where they are jointly working out how to address a regulatory question). The categoricals as stated apply to adversarial examination contexts; whether they apply with the same force to cooperative contexts is less clear. We have not developed this distinction in the paper because it complicates the categoricals without changing the load-bearing claims; we mark it here because subsequent work may need to address it.

**The categoricals' regulatory uptake is a prediction the paper makes implicitly.** The conclusion claims that frameworks built on the dishonest middle "will not survive the recognition" of regulatory scrutiny that learns to ask what an honest verification regime requires. This is a prediction about regulatory direction. The prediction is consistent with the trajectory of the FS AI RMF and with the April 2026 revised model-risk guidance, but it remains a prediction. If regulatory direction shifts — if explainability-as-Shapley-attribution becomes regulatorily entrenched, if "human in the loop" becomes a sufficient condition for AI governance approval, if the GenAI exclusion in current model-risk guidance becomes permanent rather than temporary — the categoricals may remain structurally true while becoming regulatorily orphaned. The paper's political-economy assumption is that the regulatory community is moving in the direction the categoricals point. If that movement stalls or reverses, the paper's regulatory effect may be substantially smaller than the conclusion suggests.

## Uncertainties about the diagnostic techniques

**The techniques' adoption depends on examination resources we have not analyzed.** Section 4 names the coordination problem that the techniques' value depends on examination resources, expertise, and institutional support. The paper does not analyze whether these resources are likely to be available. Examination practice is resource-constrained; not every examiner can apply techniques of this depth to every institution. The techniques work best when their existence is widely known across the regulatory community, so that institutions anticipate them and architect for them in advance, rather than relying on examiner application case by case. Whether the techniques achieve sufficient regulatory currency to produce architectural-anticipation effects, or whether they remain niche analytical tools applied by some examiners but not others, is a coordination problem the paper does not address.

**The institutional-evasion problem is real and not solved.** Sophisticated institutions can architect their systems and documentation to anticipate the diagnostic techniques while preserving the substantive gaps the techniques are meant to surface. An institution that understands pages-missing diagnosis can produce attestation patterns that appear non-suspicious without actually attesting the substantive material; an institution that understands produced-silence diagnosis can produce challenge channels that appear available without actually being usable. The defense against evasion is examiner expertise developed over time, examination practice that compounds across institutions, and continued evolution of the techniques as institutional responses develop. None of these defenses is automatic, and the techniques' effectiveness in the medium term depends on whether the regulatory community develops them as a community rather than as individual examiners working in isolation.

**The aggregate-versus-individual representation argument from the pricing turn is philosophically sharp and not fully developed.** Section 3's pricing turn argues that aggregate statistical validation does not constitute verification regime for the individual empty chair. The argument is correct as far as it goes, but it depends on accepting that individual representation has a different weight than aggregate representation, which is not obviously a position the FS AI RMF takes. We have not developed the philosophical argument that grounds the aggregate-versus-individual distinction; we have used the distinction to make the pricing-turn argument land. Whether the regulatory community accepts the distinction as substantively important is uncertain.

## Uncertainties about the architectural primitives

**The four primitives may be insufficient.** Section 5 names tamper-evident temporal capture, evidence binding, three-dimensional confidence characterization, and structural drift typology as the primitives the diagnostic techniques require. We have argued that these four are necessary; we have not argued that they are sufficient. Implementation work in Paper 2 will reveal whether additional primitives are required to support the diagnostic techniques in operational deployments. Specifically, primitives concerning model-internal observation (tensor-channel attestation, intermediate-representation logging) may emerge as necessary; primitives concerning institutional process (decision-authority routing, escalation pathway capture) may emerge as necessary. The four primitives identified here are the ones the structural argument supports; the empirical sufficiency question is open.

**The institutional-self-knowledge demotion is a structural call we may have gotten wrong.** Section 5 demoted institutional self-knowledge from primitive to emergent-property-of-the-four-primitives. The argument was that an institution operating the four primitives well accumulates institutional self-knowledge as a byproduct. This is a strong claim about emergence, and emergence claims are notoriously difficult to verify. If implementation work in Paper 2 reveals that institutional self-knowledge does not emerge robustly from the four primitives — that an architecture can satisfy the four and still fail to produce the durable institutional understanding the strawman material identified as valuable — then institutional self-knowledge needs to be promoted back to primitive status, with its own property-level specification. The demotion is open to revision; the position paper takes it as a structural call but does not foreclose its reversal.

**The primitives' operational tractability is empirical and unproven.** The paper argues for the primitives at the property level. Whether implementations of these primitives are tractable for community banks under cost and resource constraints is empirical and not addressed by the paper's structural argument. Tamper-evident temporal capture is operationally expensive; evidence binding requires storage and indexing infrastructure; three-dimensional confidence characterization complicates downstream consumption of model outputs; structural drift typology requires multiple monitoring pipelines rather than a single performance dashboard. The aggregate cost of an honest implementation may exceed what community banks can sustainably absorb, in which case the primitives' value is structural-but-aspirational rather than structural-and-deployable. The empirical question is the work of Paper 2 and of pilot deployments; the position paper's claims rest on the structural argument and stop where the empirical argument begins.

## The meta-uncertainty: theory of effect

**The paper's theory of how a position paper produces effect is itself uncertain.** Position papers can produce effect through several mechanisms: they can shape regulatory thinking before the regulation is fully developed; they can give examiners interpretive tools they apply within existing authority; they can give architects design vocabulary that propagates through the builder community; they can give the regulated community advance notice of where regulatory practice is heading, allowing institutions to architect ahead of explicit requirements. Each mechanism has different timescales, different audiences, and different success criteria.

The paper's primary intended effect is at the architectural level: shaping what gets built so that built systems are diagnosable in the ways the frame requires. The conclusion's strong claims about regulatory direction support this primary effect; the diagnostic techniques in Section 4, framed as architect-primary, support it. The paper's secondary effects — on examination practice, on institutional anticipation, on regulatory direction — depend on the primary effect succeeding well enough that examiners encounter systems built with the frame in mind, that institutions perceive the regulatory direction the paper articulates as their actual direction, that regulators read the frame as deepening their existing intuitions about FS AI RMF's purpose.

If the primary effect fails — if architects do not adopt the frame and continue building systems without the diagnostic primitives — the secondary effects also degrade. Examiners trained in the techniques apply them to systems that cannot answer the diagnostic questions, producing findings of structural inadequacy that institutions resist on grounds of incommensurability with how systems are actually built. The frame becomes academic rather than operational. The position paper has produced commitments that practice does not honor.

This is the deepest uncertainty the paper carries: whether the structural arguments developed here, however well-reasoned, can produce the practical effect they aspire to. The paper's working assumption is that the regulatory community is sufficiently aligned with the empty-chair frame's underlying intuitions that the frame, articulated and operationalized, will find purchase in practice. The assumption is reasonable but not established. The paper's effect, like the regulatory direction it argues for, is a prediction more than a result.

## What this section earns

We have named uncertainties at every level of the paper's argument. We have not resolved them. The point of the section is not to claim that the uncertainties are minor or that they will be resolved in subsequent work; the point is to mark where the paper's structural arguments stop and where empirical, political, and practical questions begin. The uncertainties are not weaknesses to be hidden; they are the boundary of what a structural argument can establish, and the boundary is where the next work begins.

The position paper's load-bearing function is to articulate structural commitments that the regulatory and architectural communities can adopt or reject, building on what the structural argument establishes and addressing what it leaves open. The uncertainties named here are the open questions; the structural arguments in Sections 1 through 5 are what the paper offers as established. We have tried to mark the boundary clearly, so that what the paper claims can be evaluated separately from what the paper hopes.
