The empty-chair frame's value depends on its generativity. If naming absent-party representation as the organizing principle behind the FS AI RMF produces only a more sympathetic vocabulary for compliance theater, the frame has earned nothing. If it produces interpretive leverage on specific controls — vocabulary for talking about what a control is for, criteria for assessing whether an implementation represents the parties the control was written to protect, diagnostic moves available to examiners reviewing the implementation — the frame has earned its position as an organizing principle. This section demonstrates the latter through a worked example in BSA/AML transaction monitoring, and in the course of doing so refines the frame itself by distinguishing two kinds of absence.

We choose BSA/AML rather than lending or underwriting deliberately. Lending is where the empty-chair frame is most obvious — the loan applicant is concrete, the harm is concrete, fair-lending controls have decades of regulatory history to draw on, and the absent party's interest is well-rehearsed. The frame applied to lending tends to confirm what readers already believe, which is a poor test of whether the frame is doing work or merely renaming work that was already being done. BSA/AML is harder. The empty chairs are plural and conflicting in ways lending is not. The institution's interest is more aligned with the regulator's than in lending. The customer's interest is ambiguous — protection from fraud and protection from false flagging point in opposite operational directions. If the frame produces clarity here, where the chairs disagree and the controls do not align cleanly with any single absent party, the frame is doing more than renaming.

## Two kinds of absence

Before enumerating the empty chairs in BSA/AML, we refine the frame. The opening movement treated absence as a single category — parties not in the room when a decision is made. In practice, absence has two structurally distinct forms, and the diagnostic moves available to address them differ.

*Structural absence* describes parties whose absence is given by the situation: parties who do not yet exist, who have no embodied agent, or whose absence is a designed feature of the regulatory regime. The future compliance officer, the not-yet-victimized customer, the financial system as such, the criminal we are designing to exclude — these are absent because the architecture of the situation places them outside the decision context. The frame's diagnostic move on structural absence is *how does the architecture make this party audible to the parties who are present?*

*Produced absence* describes parties whose absence is created by the architecture itself, then read by the architecture as evidence of correctness. The customer who would have appealed if appeal were not prohibitively expensive. The borrower who stopped applying after repeated denial and whose absence from the application data is read as evidence of self-selection out of credit demand. The reviewer whose actual reasoning is structurally erased by a documentation regime that captures only post-hoc justification, where the absence of contradicting evidence in the record is read as evidence the documentation is faithful. In each case, the architecture has costs or design choices that suppress a voice, and the architecture then interprets the suppressed voice's silence as endorsement of the decision that suppressed it.

The distinction matters because produced absence is invisible to the empty-chair frame as initially stated. *Make the absent party audible* is the wrong operation when the architecture is producing the silence — the chair appears empty because the architecture removed it, and asking how to give voice to the chair while leaving the removal-mechanism intact will fail. The diagnostic move on produced absence is *what is the architecture doing that creates this silence, and what is it inferring from the silence it created?*

This distinction sharpens position one of the paper. Compliance theater is partly the failure to represent structural absences. But compliance theater's persistence is more substantially about produced absences — about architectures that suppress challenge, then read the absence of challenge as evidence the architecture is correct. The dishonest middle is not merely a failure of representation. It is an active production of silence, interpreted as endorsement.

## The empty chairs in BSA/AML monitoring

Six absent parties have interests in how a community bank monitors transactions for suspicious activity. Each is structural, produced, or both. We mark which.

*The next victim of fraud whose pattern this transaction is part of.* Structural absence. When a transaction is part of an emerging fraud pattern, the institution's monitoring decision affects not the customer making the transaction but the customer who will be victimized in the next iteration. The next victim is fully absent from the transaction record and from the institution's customer relationship by the structure of time and the limits of prediction. They are represented in the monitoring architecture or they are not represented at all.

*The customer whose account is frozen on a false positive.* Mixed: structural and produced. The customer is present in the transaction record but absent from the decision context that determined how their pattern was scored — that absence is structural, given that the model evaluates the transaction without consulting the customer. But the customer's silence after the freeze is partly produced: appeal processes are typically opaque, expensive, and slow, and many false-positive customers absorb the cost rather than contest it. The institution's apparent flagging accuracy is sustained in part by appeal-cost dynamics that suppress challenge from the falsely flagged.

*The marginalized customer whose transaction patterns are flagged at elevated rates.* Mixed: structural and produced. Models trained on historical SAR data inherit historical flagging biases [citation needed]. Communities whose transaction patterns differ from the model's training distribution — recent immigrants using money-transfer services, cash-economy participants, members of religious or cultural communities with non-standard remittance patterns — experience flagging rates uncorrelated with their actual involvement in suspicious activity [citation needed]. The training-distribution gap is structural absence: the model was not trained to represent these populations as separate. But the appeal-cost dynamic is produced absence: the population most likely to be falsely flagged is also the population least equipped to contest the flag, and the institution's stable false-positive rate across populations is partly an artifact of differential appeal costs rather than evidence of unbiased flagging.

*The institution itself, in the form of its future enforcement exposure.* Structural absence. The bank that fails to file required SARs faces regulatory action; the bank that over-files faces examiner skepticism about its risk calibration. Both are forms of enforcement exposure that compound over time. The institution's future self — the bank in five years, when current monitoring decisions become the subject of an examination — is an empty chair the present institution can either represent through architectural attestation or fail to represent through reliance on reconstructed reasoning.

*The financial system, in the form of monitoring quality across institutions.* Structural absence. BSA/AML is a coordinated regulatory regime; one institution's monitoring inadequacy creates externalities for institutions whose monitoring is more rigorous, because criminals route around weak links [citation needed]. The system as a whole is an empty chair represented by inter-agency information sharing, FinCEN reporting, and cross-institution examination practice. The community bank's monitoring architecture either contributes to this representation or free-rides on it.

*The criminal whose laundering depends on monitoring inadequacy.* Structural absence by intentional design. We name this empty chair explicitly because its absence in the architecture is intentional and structural, and because conflating "we should not represent the criminal" with "we should not consider the criminal's interest in our threat model" is a category error. The criminal's interest is the threat the architecture is designed against. The frame requires naming the chair we are designing to exclude. We also note the boundary case: the criminal and the marginalized customer can be hard to distinguish from behavior patterns alone, and architectures that conflate the two by design produce a particularly vicious form of produced absence — the marginalized customer flagged as criminal, whose appeal is expensive and whose silence after flagging is read as confirmation of suspicion.

The 230 control objectives do not talk about controls this way. They specify what the institution must do — record-keeping, suspicious-activity reporting, customer due diligence, transaction monitoring thresholds — without naming whose interest each requirement structurally protects, and without distinguishing structural from produced absence. The empty-chair frame's first contribution is the vocabulary that makes both kinds of structure visible: each control's substance is determined by which empty chairs it represents and which it does not, and by whether the absences it leaves unrepresented are structural or produced by the control's own design.

## Walking three controls through the frame

Consider three FS AI RMF control objectives that touch transaction monitoring in different ways. We do not specify the exact control numbers — the framework's structure is more stable than its enumeration, and tying the analysis to specific numbers makes this section brittle. The substantive content is what matters.

*Control: monitoring system performance is continuously assessed for drift in detection rates and false-positive rates, with documented thresholds triggering revalidation.*

The empty-chair reading: this control protects the institution's future self (drift detection prevents enforcement exposure from gradually degraded monitoring), protects the next victim of fraud (drift detection ensures detection rates remain adequate), and partially protects the marginalized customer (false-positive drift surfaces disparate impact over time, if the false-positive rate is decomposed by population rather than aggregated). Implementation choices determine which empty chair is actually represented. A system that monitors aggregate false-positive rates without decomposition by population represents the institution and the next victim but does not represent the marginalized customer; the false-positive rate can remain stable in aggregate while shifting in distribution across populations. The control's text is satisfied; the marginalized customer's chair remains empty. An implementation that monitors decomposed false-positive rates and triggers revalidation on disparate-impact drift represents the marginalized customer; the implementation choice is the difference between a control that does its work and a control that performs compliance.

A further produced-absence consideration: the false-positive rate the system measures is itself partly an artifact of differential appeal rates. Customers who challenge false flags reduce the measured false-positive count; customers who absorb the cost of being flagged do not. A monitoring system that treats its measured false-positive rate as ground truth is reading the produced silence of customers who could not afford to appeal as evidence of correct flagging. An implementation that distinguishes contested from uncontested flags, and treats the uncontested-flag rate as evidence about appeal costs rather than as evidence about flagging accuracy, represents the produced-absence customer that the aggregate measure suppresses.

This is the frame's first interpretive move: the control's text underspecifies which empty chairs it represents, and the implementation choice is where the substantive question lives. Asking *which empty chair does this implementation represent* is more diagnostic than asking *does this implementation comply with the control text*. Asking *what silence does the implementation produce, and what does it infer from that silence* sharpens the diagnostic further.

*Control: AI components used in transaction monitoring have documented inventory entries including model purpose, training data provenance, and validation methodology.*

The empty-chair reading: this control protects the institution's future self (an examiner arriving in three years can reconstruct what the system was), protects the future compliance officer (institutional knowledge survives personnel turnover), and indirectly protects the marginalized customer (training data provenance is the precondition for assessing whether the model represents their population adequately). The control does not directly protect the false-positive customer or the next victim; their interests are downstream of model performance, which the inventory does not directly assess.

The implementation question is what counts as adequate documentation. A model card listing high-level capabilities and a training-data summary protects no empty chair effectively — it produces a documented inventory entry without producing the institutional self-knowledge the inventory is for. An entry that captures specific decisions during model development, the empty chairs considered in those decisions, the trade-offs made, and the institutional context in which the model was approved produces inventory that the future compliance officer can actually use. The first is theater; the second is representation. Both satisfy the control text. The frame produces the distinction the text does not.

*Control: human review processes for AI-flagged transactions include documentation of reviewer reasoning sufficient for later examination.*

The empty-chair reading: this control reaches for the dishonest middle, and produces a particularly clean example of produced absence. The text frames human review as the verification regime; the reviewer's documented reasoning is treated as the audit trail. The empty chairs the control is meant to protect — the false-positive customer, the next victim, the institution's future self — are represented only if the documented reasoning actually captures what the reviewer did. This is where the second categorical from the opening becomes operational: documented reasoning is not verification of reasoning unless the documentation binds to what the reviewer actually thought, and reviewer self-reports are systematically poor at this binding under time pressure and case volume [citation needed].

The produced-absence dimension: a documentation regime that asks reviewers to write justification after deciding produces narrative that satisfies the control text. The reviewer's actual contemporaneous reasoning — the considerations weighed, the alternatives entertained, the moments of uncertainty — is structurally erased, because the documentation regime did not capture it as it occurred. The institution then reads the absence of contradicting evidence in the documentation record as evidence the documentation is faithful. The reviewer's actual reasoning has been suppressed by the documentation regime, and its silence has been interpreted as endorsement of the regime's faithfulness. This is produced absence at the architectural level, and the control as commonly implemented is one of its principal mechanisms.

A review interface that captures decision context as the reviewer engages with it — what was consulted, what alternatives were considered, what made the difference — produces contemporaneous evidence that does not require the reviewer's silence to be interpreted as endorsement. The control underspecifies; the frame makes the underspecification visible; the produced-absence distinction makes visible what the underspecification costs.

## What the frame produced

Four moves, in this section, the checklist did not produce. First, an explicit accounting of which absent parties have interests in BSA/AML monitoring, including the chair we are designing to exclude. Second, a distinction between structural and produced absence, with different diagnostic moves for each. Third, a vocabulary for distinguishing controls by which empty chairs their implementations represent and which kinds of absence they leave unaddressed, separating control text from implementation substance. Fourth, a diagnostic question pair — *which empty chair does this implementation represent, and what silence does it produce that it then reads as endorsement?* — that produces leverage on assessment that the compliance question — *does this implementation satisfy the text* — does not.

These are not solutions to the problems the controls address. They are a frame for thinking about the controls that produces interpretive and diagnostic moves the framework's checklist surface does not provide. The frame's claim to organizing principle status rests on whether moves of this kind generalize across controls and across regulated decision contexts, which the remainder of this paper develops. What this section establishes is that the moves exist — that the frame produces structure where the checklist produces only enumeration, and that the structural-versus-produced distinction adds a dimension to the frame that is invisible without it.
